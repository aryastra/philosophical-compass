Section 2: Condemned to Be Free: The Weight of Choice

Existentialist: The Neuroscientist sees a chain of causation, but I see a chasm of possibility. The very fact that we can contemplate our own determinism is an act of freedom. You say my choice is a product of my past, but I can choose to rebel against my past. I can act in a way that is utterly inconsistent with my history. Even refusing to choose is a choice with its own consequences. This is the awesome responsibility of being human: we are radically free, and therefore, we are the sole artists of our own essence. There is no pre-written script; there is only the blank page and the trembling hand that must write upon it.

Al-Ghazālī: You both speak with conviction, but from opposite ends of a string, ignoring the hand that holds it. To the Neuroscientist, I ask: can your instruments measure the First Cause? You see the links in the chain, but you cannot see the anchor to which the chain is fixed. Your science is a description of *how* the world works, not *why* it exists at all. To the Existentialist, I ask: you speak of a radical freedom, but from where does this freedom arise? Does a wave choose to crash upon the shore? Does a seed choose to sprout? Every event has a cause. You claim man is the one exception, an uncaused cause. But in Islam, there is only one Uncaused Cause—God Himself. How can you claim this ultimate freedom for yourself, if God is the Creator of all things, including your will and the very actions you choose?
