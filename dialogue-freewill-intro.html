The Illusion of Choice?

A Dialogue on Freewill, Fate, and the Brain

Are you the author of your life story, or just a character reading from a script you didn't write? The question of free will is one of the most persistent and unsettling in all of philosophy.

Modern neuroscience suggests our choices are the predictable results of brain chemistry and electrical signals, a chain of cause and effect leaving little room for a 'ghost in the machine.' At the same time, existentialist philosophy argues that we are "condemned to be free"—that our freedom is so absolute, we are responsible for creating our own meaning in a universe that offers none.

But is there a third path? Classical Islamic theology introduces a profound view that seeks to balance divine decree (qadr) with human accountability (kasb). How can an all-knowing God's plan coexist with our freedom to choose?

This dialogue brings these three powerful perspectives into a single conversation. You'll hear from a Neuroscientist, an Existentialist Philosopher, and the classical Islamic theologian Al-Ghazālī.

Together, they will explore:

  • If my brain makes my decisions, am 'I' truly free?
  • What is the difference between freedom and the feeling of freedom?
  • How can divine predestination and human moral responsibility both be true?
  • Does science leave any room for a soul or a will?

Join us for a fascinating exploration into one of the oldest and most challenging questions of the human experience.
